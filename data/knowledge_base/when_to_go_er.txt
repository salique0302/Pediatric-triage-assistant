TOPIC: When to Go to Emergency Room with a Child
SOURCE: WHO/NHS/CDC (public guidelines)
LANGUAGE: en

Knowing when to seek emergency care for your child can be lifesaving. Trust your instincts — if you feel something is seriously wrong, seek help immediately.

CALL AMBULANCE OR GO TO ER IMMEDIATELY FOR:

Breathing problems:
- Difficulty breathing, gasping for air
- Blue lips, tongue, or face
- Stopped breathing or irregular breathing
- Severe wheezing or stridor
- Choking

Neurological emergencies:
- Seizure or convulsion
- Loss of consciousness or unresponsive
- Severe head injury or fall from height
- Extreme drowsiness or cannot be woken
- Confusion or unusual behavior
- Severe headache with stiff neck
- Bulging soft spot (fontanelle) in baby

Severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis):
- Difficulty breathing or swallowing
- Swelling of face, lips, or tongue
- Widespread hives with breathing difficulty
- Sudden collapse

Serious injuries:
- Severe bleeding that won't stop
- Deep cuts requiring stitches
- Suspected broken bone
- Severe burns
- Eye injury with vision changes
- Suspected poisoning or ingestion of toxic substance

Severe pain or distress:
- Severe abdominal pain (especially if child is doubled over)
- Testicular pain or swelling
- Inconsolable crying for hours in infant
- Child is limp or floppy

Signs of serious infection:
- Non-blanching rash (doesn't fade when pressed with glass)
- High fever with extreme lethargy or confusion
- Fever in baby under 3 months
- Signs of severe dehydration

Other emergencies:
- Persistent vomiting (cannot keep any fluids down for 12+ hours)
- Vomiting blood or green/yellow bile
- Blood in stool (significant amount)
- Severe allergic reaction
- Suspected appendicitis

WHEN TO SEE DOCTOR URGENTLY (same day but not emergency):
- High fever not responding to medication
- Moderate dehydration
- Persistent vomiting or diarrhea
- Ear pain with fever
- Suspected urinary tract infection
- Rash with fever
- Cough with difficulty breathing (mild to moderate)

TRUST YOUR PARENTAL INSTINCT:
If your child looks or acts very sick, or you are seriously worried, seek medical help. You know your child best.

DO NOT HESITATE to call emergency services if unsure. It is better to be cautious with a child's health.
