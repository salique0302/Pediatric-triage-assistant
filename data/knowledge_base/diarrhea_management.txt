TOPIC: Diarrhea Management in Children
SOURCE: WHO/NHS/CDC (public guidelines)
LANGUAGE: en

Diarrhea is defined as loose or watery stools occurring more frequently than normal. It is very common in children and usually caused by viral gastroenteritis.

CAUSES:
- Viral infections (most common): rotavirus, norovirus, adenovirus
- Bacterial infections: salmonella, E. coli, campylobacter
- Food intolerance or allergy
- Antibiotics (disrupts gut bacteria)
- Parasites (less common)

HOME MANAGEMENT:

Hydration is the priority:
- Continue breastfeeding if infant is breastfed
- Offer oral rehydration solution (ORS) frequently in small amounts
- For older children: water, diluted juice, broth, ORS
- Avoid sugary drinks or undiluted juice (can worsen diarrhea)
- Signs of adequate hydration: regular wet diapers, moist mouth, tears when crying

Feeding:
- Do not withhold food if child is hungry
- Continue normal diet as tolerated
- BRAT diet (bananas, rice, applesauce, toast) is gentle but not required
- Avoid fatty, spicy, or very sweet foods temporarily
- Probiotics may help shorten duration

What NOT to do:
- Do not give anti-diarrheal medications to children without doctor's advice
- Do not give antibiotics unless prescribed (most cases are viral)
- Do not give fruit juice or soda

WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR:

Urgent (same day):
- Signs of dehydration: dry mouth, no tears, sunken eyes, no wet diaper for 6+ hours
- Blood or mucus in stool
- Severe abdominal pain
- High fever (above 39°C)
- Diarrhea lasting more than 7 days
- Child under 6 months with diarrhea
- Recent travel to developing countries

Emergency (call ambulance):
- Severe dehydration: extreme lethargy, unresponsive, sunken fontanelle in babies
- Signs of shock: cold hands/feet, rapid breathing, pale/mottled skin

Most cases resolve in 5-7 days with supportive care.
