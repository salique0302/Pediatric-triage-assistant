TOPIC: Common Cold Management in Children
SOURCE: WHO/NHS/CDC (public guidelines)
LANGUAGE: en

The common cold is a viral infection of the upper respiratory tract. Children can have 6-8 colds per year, especially if they attend daycare or school.

SYMPTOMS:
- Runny or stuffy nose (clear or colored mucus)
- Sneezing
- Mild cough
- Sore throat
- Low-grade fever or no fever
- Mild headache
- Decreased appetite
- Mild fatigue
- Symptoms typically last 7-10 days

STAGES:
Days 1-3: Sore throat, sneezing, runny nose begins
Days 4-7: Nasal congestion peaks, mucus may become thicker and colored
Days 8-10: Symptoms gradually improve

Note: Colored mucus (yellow or green) does NOT automatically mean bacterial infection requiring antibiotics. It is a normal part of the cold's progression.

HOME MANAGEMENT:

For all ages:
- Rest (but normal activity if child feels up to it)
- Plenty of fluids (water, warm liquids, broth)
- Saline nasal drops or spray for congestion
- Cool mist humidifier in bedroom
- Keep child away from smoke

For babies:
- Nasal aspirator to clear mucus before feeding
- Elevate head of crib slightly (place towel under mattress, not pillow in crib)
- Continue breastfeeding or formula
- Smaller, more frequent feeds if congested

For older children:
- Warm liquids (tea, soup) can soothe throat
- Honey (1 teaspoon for children over 1 year) for cough
- Gargling with warm salt water for sore throat (if old enough)
- Paracetamol or ibuprofen for fever or discomfort

WHAT NOT TO DO:
- Do not give cough and cold medicines to children under 6 years
- Do not give aspirin to children under 16 years
- Do not give antibiotics (colds are viral, not bacterial)
- Do not use vapor rubs on babies under 2 years

WHEN TO SEE DOCTOR:
- Symptoms lasting more than 10 days without improvement
- High fever (above 39°C) or fever lasting more than 3 days
- Difficulty breathing or rapid breathing
- Wheezing
- Severe headache or facial pain (possible sinusitis)
- Ear pain
- Child under 3 months with cold symptoms
- Child appears very unwell or unusually drowsy
- Refusing to drink or signs of dehydration

PREVENTION:
- Frequent handwashing
- Avoid close contact with sick people
- Teach children to cough/sneeze into elbow
- Keep toys and surfaces clean
- Ensure adequate sleep and nutrition
